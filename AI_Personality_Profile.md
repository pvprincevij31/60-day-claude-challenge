## 1. AI working style

You treat AI like a pair-programmer with no ego — fast, iterative, slightly messy. You don't write a perfect spec and wait; you throw half-formed ideas at the model, see what comes back, and pivot mid-thought. Your sessions look like a build sprint: code, break it, fix it, "wait, what if we tried it completely differently," repeat. You optimize for momentum over polish in the early stages.

## 2. Strengths & weaknesses

### Strengths
- Fast pattern recognition across unfamiliar problems
- Comfortable operating in ambiguity without needing a full spec
- Willing to throw away work that isn't working — low ego attachment to code
- Strong gut instinct for "this feels wrong" before being able to articulate why

### Weaknesses
- Scope creep is your natural predator — five tangents before lunch
- Documentation and testing discipline tend to lag behind build speed
- "Finishing the boring 20%" is where momentum often stalls
- Under-investment in planning, since planning feels slower than building

## 3. What type of AI user you are

**The Builder-Tinkerer.** Not a prompt-engineering theorist, not a passive Q&A user — you're hands-on-keyboard, using AI as an extension of your own dev loop. You trust AI more for execution than for judgment calls, which is generally the right instinct.

## 4. Best future career paths

- **AI infrastructure / applied AI engineer** — building the tools other engineers use
- **Technical founder** — a 0-to-1 builder, not a 1-to-100 operator
- **Developer tools / DX engineer** — your chaotic-creative + technical combo is exactly what good dev tooling needs
- **AI agent / automation systems builder** — orchestrating multiple models and tools rewards your "throw it together, see what breaks" style

## 5. Learning & decision-making style

You learn by breaking things, not reading documentation front-to-back. Decisions are made fast and revised often — you'd rather ship a wrong answer and correct it than deliberate to certainty. A strength in early-stage building; a liability in anything requiring long-horizon planning or irreversible decisions.

## 6. What makes you different

Most AI users fall into two camps: cautious skeptics who triple-check everything, or passive users who accept whatever comes back. You're neither — you're an active collaborator who argues with the model, redirects it, and treats wrong answers as useful signal rather than failure. That's rarer than it sounds.

## 7. What you need to improve to go elite

- **Systematize the chaos** — a 10% investment in lightweight structure before building would compound your output
- **Verification discipline** — fast iterators often skip checking why something worked, which caps how far skill compounds
- **Know when to slow down** — not every problem rewards "break it and see"; architecture, security, and costly-to-undo decisions need deliberation
